creating deepfakes

Devising a method to examine the role of behaviour in perceiving creepiness was a harder task than I thought. I soon realized how difficult it would be to ethically create a dataset which containted both static facial images and videos of "creepy" people. My supervisor published a study which, in the pilot, established a database of "creepy" static facial images. But it's not like we could contact these individuals; some of them were listed as America's Most Wanted, and some of them were robots (and we all know how flippant robots can be).

One day, I stumbled upon a silly video of a dog, which someone had edited to sing a song, and I realized it might be possible to apply the same principles used in that video to static facial images. If they could make the dog sing, I thought to myself, perhaps I could add subtle movement to images.

For scientific reasons only, here's that video:

Anyways, I had absolutely no idea where to start in creating something like this. Fortunately, there was a public repository available for people to create these things, called deepfakes. Countless video tutorials later, I was able to take the motion patterns from a driving video of myself (see below) and synthesize them on to a static image.

Here is one of the very first that I made:

From there, I was able to make a dynamic facial image for every static one. I used three different motion patterns and randomly assigned a motion pattern to each facial image.

To be honest, I was surprised I managed to do this.
All in all, it was quite a rewarding experience when I finally figured out how it could be done, and I hope that my future research endeavours will offer me opportunities to explore coding further.
